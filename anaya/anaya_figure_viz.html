What this study measured

After the summer monsoon in September 2025, Charlie Bettigole and Bill Gilbert conducted three days of field work, using soil moisture probes and digital survey tools to measure the characteristics of five sites at Anaya Springs. Four restored arroyos (stream channels) were compared against one undisturbed control arroyo, Westward Ho. The restored arroyos have small earthen or rock check dams installed to slow water flow and increase soil moisture. Each chart below shows whether the treatment arroyos differ meaningfully from the control — and how confident we are in those differences. Significance codes appear to the right of each bar.

How to read the significance codes

The codes shown to the right of each bar in the charts compare each restored arroyo to the control using a Mann-Whitney U test — a way to ask "could this difference just be random chance?" The code in the Sig. column tells you how confident we can be that the difference is real:

***Extremely strong evidence of a real difference (less than 1-in-1000 chance it's random)
**Strong evidence (less than 1-in-100 chance it's random)
*Moderate evidence (less than 1-in-20 chance it's random)
nsNot significant — the difference could easily be random
—This is the control arroyo — everything else is compared to it

A. Soil Moisture — Sensor Readings

What this shows: Surface soil moisture (%) measured by sensors placed just above the check dams at each arroyo, with hundreds of readings per site. Each box shows the middle 50% of readings; the line inside is the median; the whiskers extend to the typical range; dots are outliers.
Key takeaway: All four restored arroyos have significantly higher surface soil moisture than the control — roughly twice as wet on average. Check dams are trapping water right at the soil surface.

B. Soil Moisture — Pit Samples

What this shows: Deeper soil moisture measured by digging pits up to 12 inches deep at each arroyo and testing the soil directly. This reveals whether moisture is penetrating below the surface — not just sitting on top.
Key takeaway: All four restored arroyos show significantly higher deep soil moisture than Westward Ho — confirming that the effect of the check dams extends well below the surface.

C. Percent Bare Ground

What this shows: The percentage of ground at each site that has no vegetation — just exposed soil. High bare ground means little plant cover; low bare ground means more vegetation has established.
Key takeaway: The control arroyo (Westward Ho) averages ~90% bare ground. All restored arroyos have meaningfully less bare ground, suggesting plants are beginning to take hold where moisture is higher.

D. Plant Family Diversity

What this shows: The number of different plant families observed at each arroyo. More families = more diverse plant community, which generally indicates a healthier ecosystem.
Key takeaway: Three of four restored arroyos show significantly more plant diversity than the control. Cairn is the exception (p = 0.055), possibly because it had the smallest moisture gains.

E. Moisture vs. Plant Diversity

What this shows: Each dot is one sampling location, plotted by its soil moisture (horizontal axis) and number of plant families found (vertical axis). The dashed line is a trend line fitted across all sites. The R² value (shown on the chart) measures how well soil moisture predicts plant diversity — it ranges from 0 (no relationship) to 1 (perfect relationship).
Key takeaway: There is a positive relationship — wetter soils tend to support more diverse plant communities (R² = 0.24). This means soil moisture accounts for about 24% of the variation in plant diversity across sites. That's a meaningful signal: other factors like soil type, seed availability, and time since restoration also play a role, but moisture is clearly part of the story. The check dams aren't just adding moisture in isolation — they're helping drive ecological recovery.
